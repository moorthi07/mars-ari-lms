Module 1 — Your Job in Physical AI

You're about to become part of something new. Physical AI is the field of teaching robots to do real-world tasks by learning from humans — people like you.

What does a TeleOp Controller do?

You control robots remotely. You drive them, move their arms, pick things up, navigate spaces. While you're doing this, the platform records everything — your movements, the robot's camera feed, all the sensor data. That recording becomes training data for AI models.

Think of it like this: you're the teacher, the robot is the student. The better you control the robot, the better the AI learns.

Why your quality matters

Smooth, consistent movements = good training data = better AI. Jerky, inconsistent movements = noisy data = AI that doesn't work well. Your job isn't just to complete tasks — it's to complete them in a way that's easy for an AI to learn from.

What you'll earn

Controllers are paid per session on the TeleOp Hub marketplace. Rates vary by task complexity. Certified controllers get access to more jobs and higher-paying assignments.

Your role in one sentence: You control robots to generate high-quality training data for Physical AI systems.

Module 2 — Getting Around the Platform

The MarsGeo platform is your workspace. Let's walk through what's on screen and what each part does.

The Main Dashboard

When you log in, you see the main dashboard. It has three main areas:

  • Left/Center — The map or simulation view. This shows where your robot is or what it's doing in simulation.
  • Right panel — Analytics, media gallery, and chat. This is where you see live data from the robot.
  • Top bar — Navigation menu. Click the menu icon to open windows for TeleOp Hub, Billing, Settings, and more.

Opening Windows

The platform uses floating windows — like apps on a desktop. You can open multiple windows at once, move them around, and resize them. Open them from the top bar menu.

The Video Panel

The video panel is at the bottom of the screen. This is where you see the robot's camera feed and your controls. It's the most important panel for a controller.

Quick Navigation Tips

  • Click the menu icon (top left) to open any window
  • Drag window title bars to move them
  • Click X on a window to close it
  • The Analytics tab (chart icon, right panel) shows live robot data
  • The Media tab (camera icon, right panel) shows recorded sessions

Module 3 — Connecting to a Robot

Before you can control anything, you need to connect to a robot. Here's how.

Step 1: Open the Robots window

Click the menu icon → Management → Robots & Devices. You'll see a list of available robots.

Step 2: Check the robot's status

Each robot shows a status indicator:

  • 🟢 Online — Robot is connected and ready
  • 🟡 Busy — Another operator is using it
  • 🔴 Offline — Robot is not connected

Step 3: Check signal quality

Look at the signal meter in the video panel. You want at least 3 bars before starting a session. Poor signal = laggy controls = bad data.

Step 4: Check the camera feed

Before touching any controls, make sure you can see the robot's camera feed clearly. If the image is black, blurry, or frozen, don't start — report the issue.

What if the robot doesn't connect?

  • Refresh the page and try again
  • Check your internet connection
  • Try a different robot if available
  • Contact support if the issue persists

Module 4 — Drive Controls

Drive controls let you move a robot from place to place. This is the most basic skill — and the most important one to do smoothly.

The Joystick

The drive joystick controls two things:

  • Forward/backward (linear velocity) — Push up to go forward, down to go backward
  • Left/right (angular velocity) — Push left to turn left, right to turn right

The further you push the joystick from center, the faster the robot moves. Start with small movements until you're comfortable.

The D-Pad

The D-pad gives you precise directional control — one direction at a time. Good for careful navigation in tight spaces.

The STOP Button

The red STOP button sends an immediate stop command. The robot stops moving. Use this whenever you're unsure — it's always safe to stop.

Why smooth movement matters

Jerky, stop-start movement creates bad training data. The AI learns from your movements — if you move smoothly and consistently, the AI learns smooth, consistent behavior. Practice moving in straight lines, smooth curves, and controlled stops.

Practice Exercise

  1. Connect to a robot in simulation (MuJoCo — free, no GPU needed)
  2. Drive forward 2 meters in a straight line
  3. Turn 90 degrees left
  4. Drive forward 2 more meters
  5. Come to a smooth stop
  6. Repeat 5 times

Module 5 — Arm & Manipulator Controls

Arm controls let you move a robot's arm and gripper. This is harder than drive controls — but it's where the most valuable training data comes from.

The Arm Joystick

Controls the arm's end-effector (the gripper/hand) in 3D space. Think of it like moving your own hand — you're controlling where the gripper goes, not each individual joint.

6-DOF Sliders

Six sliders, one for each joint of the arm. Each slider moves one joint. This gives you precise control but requires more coordination. Good for fine adjustments.

SVG Arm Control

A visual representation of the arm. Click and drag joints directly on the diagram. Intuitive for beginners.

The Gripper

Open and close the gripper using the gripper control. Most tasks involve: approach → open gripper → move to object → close gripper → lift → move to target → open gripper → release.

Joint Limits

Each joint has a safe range of motion. The platform enforces these limits automatically — you can't move a joint past its safe range. If a slider stops moving, you've hit a limit. Don't force it.

Tips for Good Arm Data

  • Move slowly and deliberately — no sudden jerks
  • Approach objects from a consistent direction every time
  • Close the gripper fully before lifting
  • Don't hover — commit to the movement

Module 6 — Voice Controls

Voice controls let you send commands to the robot by speaking. This is especially useful for language-conditioned AI training — the robot learns to associate your spoken instructions with the actions you perform.

Setting Up Voice Control

  1. Click the microphone icon in the controls panel
  2. Allow browser microphone access when prompted
  3. Speak clearly into your microphone
  4. Your speech is transcribed automatically (Whisper AI)

Live Voice Control

Live voice control sends commands in real-time as you speak. The robot responds to recognized commands immediately. Useful for hands-free operation when both hands are busy with other controls.

When to Use Voice vs Manual

  • Use voice when you want to label what you're doing ("picking up red cube", "moving to bin")
  • Use manual for precise movements that require fine motor control
  • Use both for the best training data — speak what you're doing while doing it

Good Voice Data Habits

  • Speak clearly and at normal pace
  • Describe the task, not the controls ("pick up the cube" not "move arm left")
  • Be consistent — use the same phrases for the same actions
  • Minimize background noise

Module 7 — Watching & Monitoring

Good operators don't just control — they monitor. Knowing what your robot is experiencing helps you make better decisions and catch problems early.

The Video Feed

Your primary view of the robot's world. Things to watch for:

  • Image quality — Blurry or dark images mean bad training data. Stop and fix before continuing.
  • Occlusion — Is the camera blocked? Reposition if needed.
  • Lag — If the video is delayed, your controls will feel off. Check signal strength.

The Analytics Panel

The right panel shows live charts of robot data:

  • Speed — How fast the robot is moving
  • Battery — Remaining power (stop before it hits 10%)
  • Signal strength — Connection quality
  • Joint angles — Current position of each arm joint

Reading the Signal Meter

  • 🟢 4-5 bars — Excellent. Full speed ahead.
  • 🟡 2-3 bars — Acceptable. Reduce speed, be careful.
  • 🔴 0-1 bars — Poor. Stop and wait for signal to improve.

Battery Management

Always end your session before the battery hits 15%. A robot that dies mid-task creates an incomplete episode — wasted data. Watch the battery chart and plan your session length accordingly.

Module 8 — Recording a Session

Every session you record is a potential training episode for an AI model. Good session management means your work is usable, organized, and valuable.

Starting a Session

  1. Connect to your robot (Module 3)
  2. Check video feed and signal quality (Module 7)
  3. Click "Start Session" in the session controls
  4. Give the session a descriptive name: rover-pickup-task-001
  5. Begin your task

Naming Conventions

Good names make sessions findable and usable:

  • ✅ arm-pick-red-cube-table-001
  • ✅ rover-navigate-corridor-outdoor-003
  • ❌ session1
  • ❌ test
  • ❌ my session april 17

Stopping a Session

Click "Stop Session" when the task is complete — not before. An incomplete session (task not finished) is usually not useful for training. If something goes wrong mid-task, stop the session, mark it as failed, and start a new one.

Reviewing Your Sessions

After stopping, your session appears in the Media Gallery (right panel → Media tab). You can replay it to check quality. If it looks bad (jerky, incomplete, wrong task), mark it for deletion.

Session Replay

Use the playback controls to review your session. Watch for:

  • Long pauses (idle time — bad data)
  • Jerky movements
  • Task not completed
  • Camera blocked or blurry

Module 9 — Safety & Good Habits

Safety isn't just about not breaking hardware. It's about producing data that's actually useful. Bad habits create bad data. Good habits create good data — and keep everyone safe.

The Golden Rules

  1. Always check before you move. Look at the camera feed. Know where the robot is before sending any command.
  2. Start slow. Reduce speed to 30% when starting a new task or environment. Increase only when comfortable.
  3. Emergency stop first. If anything looks wrong, hit STOP immediately. Investigate before continuing.
  4. Never leave a running robot unattended. If you need to step away, stop the session first.
  5. Respect joint limits. Don't try to force a joint past its limit. You'll damage the robot.

What Makes a "Good" Session

  • ✅ Task completed from start to finish
  • ✅ Smooth, deliberate movements throughout
  • ✅ No long pauses or idle time
  • ✅ Clear camera feed throughout
  • ✅ Consistent approach to the task

What Makes a "Bad" Session

  • ❌ Task not completed
  • ❌ Jerky, stop-start movements
  • ❌ Long pauses (operator distracted)
  • ❌ Camera blocked or blurry
  • ❌ Robot hit something or got stuck

Idle Detection

The platform automatically detects when a robot hasn't moved for more than 30 seconds and will warn you. If you need to pause, stop the session — don't leave the robot sitting idle while recording.

Reporting Issues

If you notice anything wrong — hardware behaving unexpectedly, camera issues, connection problems — stop your session and report it through the platform. Don't try to work around hardware problems.

Module 10 — Finding Work on TeleOp Hub

TeleOp Hub is the marketplace where robot operators find paid work. Companies and researchers post jobs, you apply, and if accepted, you complete the sessions and get paid.

Opening TeleOp Hub

Click the menu icon → TeleOp Hub. The dashboard shows available jobs, your applications, and your completed work.

Job Types

  • Teleop — Standard robot operation. Drive, navigate, perform tasks.
  • Data Collection — Specific data collection tasks. Usually more structured with exact instructions.
  • Testing — Test a trained AI policy. Report what works and what doesn't.

Applying to a Job

  1. Browse the job list
  2. Click a job to see full details (task description, robot type, pay rate, duration)
  3. Click "Apply" and write a short cover note if required
  4. Wait for acceptance (usually within 24 hours)

Completing a Job

  1. Read the job instructions carefully before starting
  2. Complete the required number of sessions
  3. Submit your completed sessions through the job interface
  4. Payment is processed after the client reviews your work

Building Your Reputation

Clients rate your work. High-quality sessions = high ratings = more job offers = higher pay. The best controllers get invited to exclusive, higher-paying jobs. Your certification from this course is your first credential.
