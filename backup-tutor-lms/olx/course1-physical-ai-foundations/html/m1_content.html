Module 1 — What Is Physical AI?

AI has left the browser. For decades, AI meant software — recommendation engines, language models, image classifiers. Now it's moving into the physical world: robots that pick, carry, assemble, and assist. This is Physical AI.

The Paradigm Shift

Classical robotics is programmed. An engineer writes explicit rules: "if object is at position X, move arm to Y, close gripper." This works in controlled factory environments where everything is predictable. It breaks the moment the world changes — different lighting, a slightly misplaced object, a new task.

Physical AI is learned. Instead of programming behaviors, you collect demonstrations of the behavior you want, and train a model to reproduce it. The robot learns from data, not from hand-coded rules. This makes it far more adaptable.

Classical Robotics Physical AI
Hand-coded rulesLearned from data
Brittle to changeGeneralizes to variation
Modular pipeline (perceive → plan → act)End-to-end policy (observe → act)
Requires robotics expertRequires data + ML expertise
Scales with engineering hoursScales with data

VLM vs VLA — What's the Difference?

VLM (Vision-Language Model)

A model that understands images and text together. It can answer questions about what it sees, describe scenes, identify objects. Examples: GPT-4V, Gemini Vision, LLaVA. VLMs are used in robotics for perception and reasoning — understanding the scene, interpreting instructions, identifying what to pick up.

VLA (Vision-Language-Action Model)

A VLM that also outputs actions. Instead of just describing what it sees, it decides what the robot should do. Input: camera image + language instruction. Output: joint commands or end-effector movements. Examples: OpenVLA, Gemini Robotics, π0 (Pi Zero). VLAs are the end-to-end brain of a Physical AI robot.

Key insight: VLMs understand the world. VLAs act in it. A VLA is essentially a VLM with an action head added.

Why Data Is the Real Bottleneck

The models exist. The simulators exist. The hardware exists. What's scarce is high-quality robot training data. Unlike internet text or images, robot data must be:

  • Physically embodied (tied to a specific robot's sensors and actuators)
  • Temporally synchronized (camera frames aligned with joint states)
  • Task-specific (demonstrations of the exact behavior you want)
  • Consistent (same task performed the same way, repeatedly)

This is why teleoperation — humans controlling robots to generate demonstrations — is currently the primary method for collecting Physical AI training data. Companies like Physical Intelligence, Figure AI, and 1X employ teams of "robot trainers" whose job is exactly this.

Real-World Examples

  • Tesla Optimus — humanoid trained on human video + teleoperation data
  • Figure AI — manipulation tasks learned from human demonstrations
  • Physical Intelligence (π0) — generalist robot policy trained on diverse teleop data
  • Hugging Face LeRobot — open-source framework for robot learning from teleop
  • Open X-Embodiment — Google's cross-robot dataset with 1M+ demonstrations
