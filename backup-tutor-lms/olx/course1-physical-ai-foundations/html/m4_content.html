Module 4 — Teleoperation

Teleoperation is how Physical AI gets its training data. A human operator controls the robot, performing the task correctly, while every observation and action is recorded. This is called imitation learning from demonstrations — and the quality of your teleop directly determines the quality of your policy.

Why Teleoperation?

Robots can't learn from scratch in the real world — it's too slow and too dangerous. But they can learn from watching humans. Teleoperation lets you:

  • Generate expert demonstrations efficiently
  • Cover diverse task variations (different object positions, lighting, speeds)
  • Provide recovery demonstrations (what to do when things go wrong)
  • Scale data collection by hiring multiple operators

The Episode Structure

Every teleoperation session is organized into episodes. An episode is one complete attempt at a task — from reset to completion (or failure).

Episode {
  id: "ep_0042"
  task: "pick up red cube and place in bin"
  frames: [
    { t: 0.00, obs: {image: ..., joints: [...]}, action: [0.01, 0.0, ...] },
    { t: 0.02, obs: {image: ..., joints: [...]}, action: [0.02, 0.0, ...] },
    ...
    { t: 4.80, obs: {image: ..., joints: [...]}, action: [0.0, 0.0, ...] }
  ]
  success: true
  duration_seconds: 4.8
}

What Makes a Good Demonstration?

Research shows that demonstration quality matters more than quantity. A policy trained on 50 clean episodes often outperforms one trained on 500 noisy ones.

Good demonstrations are:

  • ✅ Smooth — No jerky movements or sudden stops
  • ✅ Consistent — Same approach to the task every time
  • ✅ Complete — Full task from start to finish, no partial episodes
  • ✅ Diverse — Cover different object positions, orientations, speeds
  • ✅ Recoverable — Include some recovery from near-failures

Bad demonstrations cause:

  • ❌ Idle trajectories — Operator paused, robot stood still. Policy learns to do nothing.
  • ❌ Jitter — Shaky control. Policy learns oscillating behavior.
  • ❌ Over-correction — Operator corrected too aggressively. Policy learns unstable behavior.
  • ❌ Mode collapse — All episodes look identical. Policy can't generalize.

Safety During Teleoperation

  • Always set joint limits before starting a session
  • Have an emergency stop accessible at all times
  • Start slow — reduce max speed until you're comfortable
  • Never leave a running robot unattended
  • If the robot behaves unexpectedly, stop immediately and investigate

Human-in-the-Loop (HIL) Data Collection

Advanced technique: deploy a partially-trained policy, let it run autonomously, and have a human intervene only when it's about to fail. The intervention data (recovery movements) is recorded and added to the training set. This produces policies that know how to recover from mistakes — not just how to succeed from scratch.

HIL Loop: Policy runs → Human watches → Human intervenes on failure → Recovery recorded → Policy fine-tuned → Repeat
