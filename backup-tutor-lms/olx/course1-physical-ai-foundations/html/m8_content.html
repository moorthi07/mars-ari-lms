Module 8 — Sim-to-Real & The Full Loop

A policy that works in simulation but fails on real hardware is useless. The sim-to-real gap is the final challenge in Physical AI. This module covers how to close it — and how to run the full iteration loop that continuously improves your robot.

The Sim-to-Real Gap

Simulation is an approximation of reality. The gap between them causes policies to fail when deployed on real hardware. Main sources:

Gap SourceWhat HappensFix
Physics mismatchFriction, contact, mass differ from simSystem identification, domain randomization
Visual mismatchReal camera looks different from sim renderPhotorealistic rendering, visual domain randomization
Sensor noiseReal sensors have noise; sim is perfectAdd Gaussian noise to sim observations
Control latencyReal hardware has delays; sim is instantAdd action delay in sim (1-3 timesteps)
Unmodeled dynamicsCable drag, motor heating, wearCollect real data to supplement sim

Domain Randomization

Domain randomization is the primary technique for closing the sim-to-real gap. On every episode reset, randomize:

  • Object positions and orientations (±5cm, ±30°)
  • Object colors and textures
  • Lighting direction and intensity
  • Camera position (small perturbations)
  • Physics parameters (friction ±20%, mass ±10%)
  • Background textures

The policy trained on randomized sim data learns to be robust to variation. When deployed on real hardware, the real environment is just another sample from the distribution the policy was trained on.

Safety Layers

Never deploy a policy directly to real hardware without safety constraints:

Policy output → Safety filter → Robot

The safety filter enforces:

  • Joint limits — Clip actions to safe joint ranges
  • Velocity limits — Cap maximum joint speed
  • Workspace limits — Prevent end-effector from leaving safe zone
  • Force limits — Stop if contact force exceeds threshold
  • Emergency stop — Human override always available

The Full Iteration Loop

Physical AI is not a one-shot process. It's an iterative loop:

1. Collect teleop demonstrations (50-100 episodes)
2. Train baseline policy (behavior cloning)
3. Evaluate in simulation (measure success rate)
4. Identify failure modes (what does it get wrong?)
5. Collect more data targeting failure modes
6. Retrain with augmented dataset
7. Deploy to real hardware (with safety layer)
8. Collect HIL data (human corrects real failures)
9. Fine-tune on HIL data
10. Repeat from step 3

Each iteration targets the current policy's weakest points. After 3-5 iterations, most manipulation tasks reach 80%+ success rate.

Where the Field Is Going

  • Foundation models for robotics — Large pretrained models (like GPT for language) that can be fine-tuned for any robot task
  • Synthetic data at scale — Generate millions of episodes in photorealistic sim, reducing real data requirements
  • Autonomous data collection — Robots that collect their own training data with minimal human supervision
  • Cross-embodiment transfer — Policies that work across different robot types
  • Internet-scale pretraining — Training on human video from the internet to bootstrap robot learning
